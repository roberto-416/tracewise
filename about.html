Tracewise
Transit, made legible.

Tracewise is a static reference for understanding complex urban transit systems as systems — stopping patterns, frequencies, through-running relationships, and station/interchange structure. It is for railfans, transit students, and curious tourists who want comprehension over wayfinding. It is not a trip planner, not a real-time arrival board, and not a fare calculator.

Data sources

v1 covers Osaka. The eight operators in scope are Osaka Metro, JR West, Hankyu, Hanshin, Keihan, Kintetsu, Nankai, and Kobe Rapid Transit. v1 ships seed data for Osaka Metro only; the other operators are present as placeholders so the schema is exercised end-to-end.

  • Operator timetables, station codes, and line colours: official operator publications.
  • Station coordinates: approximated for visualisation. To be refined against OpenStreetMap by the build pipeline.
  • Service frequencies: typical-day estimates, not real-time.

Methodology

Three-level model: track / line / service. A track is a physical corridor owned by one operator. A line is a passenger-facing brand composed of one or more tracks. A service is one stopping pattern that travels along a sequence of tracks. This separation is what lets Tracewise show through-running relationships clearly: when a service crosses an operator boundary, the path-across-lines view marks the transition explicitly.

Frequencies aggregate at the stop. A station's tph in a given band is the sum of all services that include it in their stopping pattern for that band. Through-services are not double-counted because each through-service is a single record, not two.

Clusters group nearby and connected nodes. A station like Umeda groups together the Midosuji Umeda, Tanimachi Higashi-Umeda, and Yotsubashi Nishi-Umeda nodes (plus Hankyu, Hanshin, and JR Osaka when their data is added). The cluster is built from a hand-curated list, the inter-station transfer graph, and a 250 m proximity rule.

FAQ

Why is "Umeda" listed as one station when it's actually three? Because as a passenger you treat them as one interchange. The transfer page shows the underlying separate stations and the walks between them.

Why no real-time data? Out of scope for v1. The static, file-based architecture is part of the goal: a reference, not a wayfinding tool.

Why are private operators (Hankyu, Hanshin, etc.) empty? Their data is not public via GTFS. Adding them requires curation from operator timetables and Wikipedia, which is the next milestone.
